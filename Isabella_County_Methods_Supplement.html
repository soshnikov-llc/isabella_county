Health Burden from Non-Communicable Diseases, Substance Use Disorders, and Mental Health Conditions in Isabella County, Michigan: A Mixed-Source Modeled Estimate

Cross-sectional analysis for community health planning, 2026
Sergey Soshnikov, MD, PhD1,2
1 Department of Public Health, Herbert H. and Grace A. Dow College of Health Professions, Central Michigan University, Mount Pleasant, MI 48859
2 Soshnikov, Michigan
Correspondence: ssoshmail@gmail.com
Version 4.0 (2025 Data Update) June 2026 Not peer-reviewed Planning purposes only Michigan LE 78.6 yrs (MDHHS 2024) CDC PLACES 2024 · CDC WONDER 2020–22
Abstract

Background. County-level disease burden estimates are rarely available in the United States outside large academic centers. This analysis applies standard DALY methodology to estimate disease burden of seven major condition groups in Isabella County, Michigan — a rural county of 64,565 residents with a designated Medical Underserved Area status and multiple Health Professional Shortage Area (HPSA) designations.

Methods. We constructed disability-adjusted life year (DALY) estimates from secondary data: CDC WONDER 2020–2022 (county mortality with rural adjustment factors), CDC PLACES 2024 (prevalence), WHO/IHME 2021 (disability weights), and U.S. Census ACS 2022 (population denominators). YLL used Michigan observed life expectancy of 78.6 years (MDHHS 2024, avg Male 76.2 / Female 80.9). Economic burden was quantified by human capital (DALYs × Michigan GDP per capita) and value of statistical life (HHS 2026, income-adjusted).

Results. Total estimated burden: 11,710 DALYs (±20%), comprising 5,067 YLL + 6,643 YLD. Leading conditions: mental health (2,713 — #1), cancer (2,693), substance use disorders (2,516). Mental health now ranks first driven by CDC PLACES 2024 showing 29.8% adult prevalence. Stroke mortality remains 49% above Michigan average. Economic burden: ~$726M/year (human capital) to ~$4.9B/year (VSL).

Limitations. Mixed-source modeled planning scenario — not a validated county burden estimate. Prevalence inputs are modeled by CDC PLACES (MLRP methodology). YLL uses simplified mean-age approach. Rural adjustment factors (×1.3–1.85) applied to state-level mortality rates for suppressed county cells. Results are directional planning approximations only.

Conclusion. Isabella County's burden is led by mental health access deficits (29.8% prevalence, 176,938:1 psychiatrist ratio), cancer, and substance use. Findings support telepsychiatry/collaborative care, AI-assisted cancer screening, MAT telehealth, and hypertension control as priorities. See ROI analysis → for investment return estimates.

1. Background and Study Context

Isabella County is a rural county in mid-Michigan (FIPS 26073), home to Central Michigan University (CMU) in Mount Pleasant. Population: 64,565 (Census ACS 2022). The presence of a large university creates an unusual demographic: substantial young adult population and elevated poverty rates under standard definitions that do not distinguish student poverty from structural community poverty.

Federal access designations: Primary Care HPSA (low-income), Dental HPSA (low-income), Mental Health HPSA (Central Michigan Service Area, multi-county), and Medically Underserved Area/Population (MUA/P) for low-income residents.

MDHHS 2020 PCNA ranks: Overall Rank 48 of 83 Michigan counties; Health Status Rank 50 of 83 (rank 1 = best). These are distinct indices — see Section 4.5. All MDHHS PCNA data are pre-pandemic baseline; post-COVID values may differ substantially.

Important framing: This document is a mixed-source modeled planning scenario. It pools data from CDC WONDER 2020–22, CDC PLACES 2024, WHO/IHME 2021, and Census ACS 2022. It should not be cited as an official county burden estimate. A publication-grade analysis requires age-specific CDC WONDER death distributions, direct county prevalence, and systematic uncertainty quantification (see Section 7). Version 4.0 incorporates CDC PLACES 2024 prevalence data, which substantially increased mental health estimates (29.8% adult prevalence vs. ~15% in prior versions).

2. Data Sources

2.1 Mortality data

Cause-specific mortality from CDC WONDER 2020–2022 (3-year pooled counts, Isabella County, FIPS 26073), with rural adjustment factors applied for suppressed cells (<10 deaths). Rural adjustment factors range from ×1.3 (diabetes) to ×1.85 (SUD), derived from MDHHS rural-urban mortality differentials. For all-cause comparison, MDHHS 2020 PCNA rate (819.9/100k) is used.

County mortality data for several causes remain suppressed in CDC WONDER (cell count <10). State-level rates with rural adjustments were applied as proxies for suppressed cells. All substitutions are explicitly flagged in results.

2.2 Prevalence data

CDC PLACES 2024 (county model-based estimates) provides primary prevalence inputs. CDC PLACES uses multilevel regression and poststratification (MLRP) applied to BRFSS data — all prevalence inputs are classified as modeled, not directly observed. Derived estimates (marked derived) used where CDC PLACES does not report the specific condition (SUD, CVD).

2.3 Disability weights and life expectancy

Disability weights: WHO/IHME GBD 2021. Moderate-severity weights applied as primary estimates.

Reference life expectancy used for YLL: 78.6 years (Michigan 2024: Male 76.2, Female 80.9, average). Source: MDHHS Vital Statistics. lifeUSMI.asp →

Cancer DW: Weighted composite DW ≈ 0.294 constructed from MDHHS 2020 site distribution: lung (0.279), colorectal (0.167), prostate (0.126), breast (0.049), other sites (0.400).

2.4 Population denominators

Total population 64,565; adult population 52,297 (≥18 yrs) (Census ACS 5-yr 2022). For conditions where CDC PLACES reports adult-only prevalence (COPD, stroke, mental health, CVD, SUD), adult population was used as the base. For population-wide conditions (diabetes, cancer), the full 64,565 was used.

2.5 Economic parameters

Human capital: DALYs × $62,000 (Michigan GDP per capita 2024). VSL: HHS ASPE 2026 (central $13.4M), income-adjusted for Isabella County using elasticity 0.4 (Viscusi & Aldy 2003): $13.4M × (46,000/80,000)^0.4 = $10.5M per statistical life (county median income $46,000 per ACS 2022). See ROI analysis for intervention-level return estimates.

3. Analytic Methods

3.1 DALY framework

Standard WHO DALY formula, no age-weighting, no discounting:

DALY = YLL + YLD (1)

3.2 YLL calculation

YLL = Deaths × (L − Ā) (2)

L = 78.6 years (Michigan LE 2024, MDHHS); Ā = mean age at death (condition-specific). Deaths estimated as (WONDER rate × rural adj.) / 100,000 × population.

YLL simplification. Standard GBD YLL = Σa d(a) × ℓ(a) (age-specific deaths × remaining LE from reference table). The formula above assumes all deaths in a cause group occur at a single mean age — a planning approximation. CDC WONDER can provide age-specific county deaths for a proper computation. Mean ages used: Mental Health 46 yrs; Cancer 67 yrs; SUD 44 yrs; CVD 72 yrs; COPD 73 yrs; Stroke 73 yrs; Diabetes 70 yrs (national CDC WONDER 2019–21 aggregates).

3.3 YLD calculation

YLD = P × DW (3)

P = prevalent cases (CDC PLACES 2024 prevalence × population base); DW = WHO/IHME GBD 2021 disability weight (moderate severity).

3.4 Economic burden

Two methods with different conceptual frameworks: Human Capital captures productivity losses only; VSL reflects societal willingness to pay to prevent deaths. The ~6–7× difference between central estimates is methodologically expected, not an error. For grant applications use VSL (aligned with HHS/OMB); for academic GBD comparison use Human Capital.

4. Analytic Considerations

4.1 Mental Health rank shift (v3 → v4)

The most significant change from the previous version (v3.0: 7,696 total DALYs) to this version (v4.0: 11,710 total DALYs) is the mental health estimate. CDC PLACES 2024 reports 29.8% adult prevalence of mental health conditions in Isabella County, compared to the ~15% estimate used previously (SAMHSA national proxy). This shift — from #4 to #1 — reflects more granular local data from the 2024 PLACES release. The earlier estimate was based on national averages; the new figure is county-specific modeled estimate. Analysts should note that CDC PLACES MH prevalence includes depression, anxiety, and other common conditions — a broader definition than severe mental illness.

4.2 Rural adjustment factors for mortality

Several cause-specific county mortality rates are suppressed in CDC WONDER (<10 deaths/3-yr pooled). Rural adjustment factors were applied to state-level rates: SUD ×1.85, Mental Health ×1.40, CVD ×1.30, COPD ×1.35, Stroke ×1.25, Diabetes ×1.30, Cancer ×1.20. These factors are derived from MDHHS rural-urban mortality analyses and represent the average excess mortality in rural Michigan counties vs. state average. They introduce uncertainty (±30% for suppressed cells) that compounds the overall ±20% DALY uncertainty estimate.

4.3 Mortality rate type — age-adjusted vs. crude

If MDHHS PCNA mortality rates are age-adjusted (common for geographic comparison dashboards), converting them to death counts by multiplying by county population is not mathematically valid — age-adjusted rates use a reference population structure, not the actual county structure. Death count estimates should be treated as indicative only. This should be resolved before publication-grade use.

4.4 Poverty rate discrepancy

Three estimates exist for Isabella County: MDHHS 2020 PCNA 26.5% below 100% FPL; Census ACS 5-yr 2024: 21.9%; Census QuickFacts 2020–24: 19.0%. Sources differ in ACS vintage, reference year, and student-population treatment. CMU's ~14,000+ enrollment substantially inflates standard ACS poverty counts. All three estimates remain well above Michigan average.

4.5 Psychiatrist shortage ratio

The 176,938:1 ratio is a HRSA HPSA designation metric — FTE-weighted for the Central Michigan Service Area (multi-county). It exceeds Isabella County's total population (64,565) because the denominator is an administrative service-area construction, not a county headcount. Interpret as a severe shortage designation indicator, not a literal one-psychiatrist-per-176,938 county ratio.

6. Discussion

The most significant finding in the 2025 data update is the emergence of mental health as the #1 DALY burden condition in Isabella County, driven by CDC PLACES 2024 reporting 29.8% adult prevalence — nearly double the prior estimate. This shift from #4 to #1 is methodologically important: it reflects a change in the underlying prevalence data source (SAMHSA national proxy → CDC PLACES county-modeled), not necessarily a true change in community health. Both estimates represent modeled values, but the CDC PLACES estimate is county-specific and should be considered more accurate for local planning.

Cancer remains the second-largest burden (2,693 DALYs) with the highest absolute YLL component (1,509), reflecting 130 deaths per year at mean age 67. Substance use disorders remain the third-highest burden and continue to have the highest years lost per death (34.6 years remaining at mean age 44).

The total DALY estimate of 11,710 represents a 52% increase from the prior version (7,696), attributable primarily to the mental health prevalence revision (+1,582 DALYs) and updated cancer and SUD estimates from newer CDC WONDER data. All DALY estimates carry ±20% uncertainty.

Using Michigan's observed life expectancy (78.6 yrs) rather than the WHO/IHME normative standard (88.9 yrs) is more policy-relevant for local planning because it prioritizes conditions causing the most years lost relative to what residents actually live. Under WHO/IHME standard, SUD would rise further given deaths at age 44 vs. LE 88.9 = 44.9 years remaining.

7. Recommendations for Future Analysis

1. Age-specific YLL. Query CDC WONDER for Isabella County deaths by 5-year age group and ICD-10 cause (3–5 year pool, e.g., 2020–2024). Apply WHO reference life table for proper age-specific YLL.

2. Post-COVID mortality update. MDHHS 2020 PCNA predates pandemic and the recent opioid mortality surge. Incorporate 2023–2024 county data when available.

3. Severity-stratified YLD. Split prevalence into mild/moderate/severe using GBD severity distributions instead of single moderate-severity DW.

4. Mental health validation. The 29.8% MH prevalence (CDC PLACES 2024) warrants triangulation against BRFSS, SAMHSA NSDUH state estimates, and administrative claims data to confirm county-level estimate is not an artifact of model extrapolation.

5. Uncertainty quantification. Monte Carlo propagation of input uncertainty (mortality rate, rural adj. factor, prevalence, DW) to produce 95% uncertainty intervals at condition level.

6. Student population sensitivity. Poverty and MH analysis with and without 18–24 CMU population to separate student vs. structural community burden.
