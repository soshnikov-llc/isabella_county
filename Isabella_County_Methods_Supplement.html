Disease Burden of Non-Communicable Conditions, Substance Use Disorders, and Mental Health Disorders in Isabella County, Michigan: A Mixed-Source Modeled Estimate Using Global Burden of Disease Methodology

Cross-sectional analysis for community health planning, 2026
Sergey Soshnikov, MD, PhD1,2
1 Department of Public Health, Herbert H. and Grace A. Dow College of Health Professions, Central Michigan University, Mount Pleasant, MI 48859
2 Soshnikov Consulting LLC, Michigan
Correspondence: biososh@gmail.com
Version 3.0 (Audit-Revised) · June 2026 · For planning and methodological review purposes · Not a peer-reviewed publication

Abstract

Background. County-level disease burden estimates are rarely available in the United States outside large academic centers. This analysis applies Global Burden of Disease (GBD) methodology to estimate the disease burden of seven major condition groups in Isabella County, Michigan — a rural county of 64,565 residents with a designated Medical Underserved Area status and multiple Health Professional Shortage Area (HPSA) designations.

Methods. We constructed disability-adjusted life year (DALY) estimates from secondary data sources: Michigan MDHHS 2020 Primary Care Needs Assessment (mortality rates), CDC PLACES/BRFSS (prevalence), IHME/WHO 2023 (disability weights, reference life table), and U.S. Census ACS 2024 (population denominators). YLL was estimated using a simplified single mean-age-at-death approach. YLD was estimated as prevalent cases × internationally validated disability weight (IHME/WHO 2023) (moderate severity). Economic burden was quantified by two methods: human capital (DALYs × Michigan GDP per capita) and value of statistical life (HHS 2026, income-adjusted).

Results. Total estimated disease burden is 7,696 DALYs (uncertainty range ±20%), decomposed into 3,286 YLL and 4,410 YLD. The leading burden conditions are cancer (1,585 DALYs), substance use disorders (1,491), and cardiovascular disease (1,205). Stroke mortality is 49% above the state average. The psychiatrist-to-population ratio under HRSA HPSA methodology is 18.9× worse than the state average. Economic burden ranges from ~$477M (human capital) to ~$4.1B (VSL central estimate).

Limitations. This is a mixed-source modeled planning scenario, not a validated county burden estimate. Several county mortality values are suppressed in CDC WONDER and replaced with state-level proxies. Prevalence inputs are modeled, not directly observed at the county level. YLL calculations use simplified assumptions. Results should be interpreted as directional planning approximations. A publication-grade analysis would require age-specific CDC WONDER death distributions and severity-split prevalence inputs.

Conclusion. Isabella County carries a substantial and largely preventable disease burden, driven by poverty, limited mental health access, high tobacco use, and substance use disorders. These findings support prioritization of telepsychiatry expansion, hypertension control, tobacco cessation, and prenatal substance use disorder treatment.

Keywords: disability-adjusted life years; disease burden; rural health; Michigan; Global Burden of Disease; public health planning; health professional shortage areas

1. Background and Study Context

Isabella County is a rural county in mid-Michigan (FIPS 26073), home to Central Michigan University (CMU) in Mount Pleasant. The county population was 64,565 as of the 2024 American Community Survey 5-year estimate. The presence of a large university creates a demographic composition unusual for rural Michigan: a substantial young adult population and correspondingly elevated poverty rates under standard poverty definitions that do not separate student poverty from structural community poverty.

The county carries several federal designations reflecting healthcare access barriers: a Primary Care Health Professional Shortage Area (HPSA) for low-income populations, a Dental HPSA for low-income populations, and a Mental Health HPSA for the Central Michigan Service Area (a multi-county service region). It is also designated a Medically Underserved Area/Population (MUA/P) for low-income residents.

The MDHHS 2020 Primary Care Needs Assessment assigned Isabella County an Overall Rank of 48 of 83 Michigan counties (where rank 1 = best) and a Health Status Rank of 50 of 83. These ranks reflect a composite of health outcome and social determinant indicators measured prior to the COVID-19 pandemic. Post-pandemic values may differ substantially, particularly for substance use disorder and mental health indicators.

Important framing: This document is a mixed-source modeled planning scenario. It pools data from multiple source years (MDHHS 2020, WHO DALY methodology, County Health Rankings 2025, Census ACS 2024–2025) and uses simplified DALY methods. It should not be cited as an official county burden estimate. A fully observed county estimate would require age-specific CDC WONDER mortality distributions, direct county prevalence data, and systematic uncertainty quantification. That analysis is identified as a priority for future work in Section 7.

2. Data Sources

2.1 Mortality data

Cause-specific mortality rates were obtained from the Michigan Department of Health and Human Services (MDHHS) 2020 Primary Care Needs Assessment (PCNA), Isabella County Profile (Overall Rank 48 of 83). Rates are expressed per 100,000 population and reflect data collected prior to the COVID-19 pandemic. The PCNA does not specify whether displayed rates are age-adjusted or crude, which has implications for the validity of converting rates to estimated death counts (see Section 4.3).

County-level mortality data for substance use disorders, suicide, and several other conditions are suppressed in CDC WONDER because cell counts fall below the 10-death threshold. For substance use disorders, the Michigan state rate (29.3/100,000) was substituted as a proxy. Suicide-attributed YLL was assigned zero in this analysis. These substitutions introduce imprecision and are explicitly flagged in all results.

2.2 Prevalence data

Condition-specific prevalence estimates were derived from the following sources, in priority order: CDC PLACES (county-level model-based estimates for diabetes, obesity, and several NCD indicators); BRFSS state-level estimates (substance use disorders); published literature and national SAMHSA estimates (substance use disorders, mental health); and MDHHS PCNA figures. Because CDC PLACES derives county-level estimates through multilevel regression and post-stratification rather than direct county surveys, all prevalence inputs in this analysis are classified as modeled rather than directly observed.

2.3 Disability weights

All disability weights were drawn from the IHME Global Burden of Disease 2021 study (WHO DALY methodology), which is the most recent complete GBD cycle as of the time of this analysis (published October 2025 in The Lancet). We applied moderate-severity disability weights for each condition as the primary estimate, reflecting the expected central severity in a general population with access to some care. Sensitivity to severity assumption is acknowledged but not formally analyzed here.

An exception applies to cancer: WHO DALY methodology does not publish a single aggregate disability weight for all malignant neoplasms. We constructed a weighted composite (DW ≈ 0.30) based on the cancer-site distribution reported in MDHHS 2020 for Isabella County: lung/bronchus cancer (DW 0.279), colorectal (0.167), prostate (0.126), and breast cancer (0.049). This approximation introduces meaningful uncertainty into the cancer YLD estimate.

2.4 Population denominators

The total county population (64,565) is from the Census ACS 5-year estimate (2024). For conditions where CDC PLACES reports adult-only prevalence (COPD, stroke, mental health disorders), an adult population denominator of approximately 52,000 was used (persons ≥18 years, derived from Census ACS 2024 age distribution). For conditions where prevalence applies to the total population (diabetes, cancer, SUD), the full population of 64,565 was used.

2.5 Economic parameters

Human capital economic burden was calculated as total DALYs × Michigan GDP per capita ($62,000, 2024). Value of Statistical Life estimates were drawn from HHS ASPE Standard Values for Regulatory Analysis, 2026 (central: $13.4M; low: $6.2M; high: $20.3M per statistical life). The national VSL was adjusted for Isabella County income using an income elasticity of 0.4 (Viscusi and Aldy 2003), yielding a county-adjusted central VSL of $11.6M: $13.4M × (55,237 / 80,000)^0.4.

3. Analytic Methods

3.1 DALY framework

We used the standard GBD DALY formula with no age-weighting and no discounting, consistent with WHO DALY methodology practice:

DALY = YLL + YLD (1)

where YLL (Years of Life Lost) captures premature mortality burden and YLD (Years Lived with Disability) captures morbidity burden.

3.2 YLL calculation

YLL was estimated using the following simplified formula:

YLL = Deaths × (L − Ā) (2)

where L = 78.6 years (Michigan observed LE 2024: Male 76.2, Female 80.9, MDHHS) and Ā = assumed mean age at death for the condition. Annual deaths were estimated as D = (rate / 100,000) × population.

Methodological limitation — YLL simplification. The standard GBD YLL calculation sums over age-specific deaths: YLL = Σa d(a) × ℓ(a), where d(a) = deaths at age a and ℓ(a) = remaining life expectancy from the reference life table at age a. CDC WONDER provides county-level deaths by 5-year age group, which would permit this calculation. The simplified formula in Equation (2) is a planning approximation that assumes all deaths in a cause category occur at a single mean age. This can over- or underestimate true YLL depending on the age distribution of deaths. Future analyses should use age-specific CDC WONDER mortality data.

Mean ages at death used in this analysis are national estimates from CDC WONDER 2019–2021 aggregate data (CVD: 72 yrs; Cancer: 68 yrs; SUD: 45 yrs; COPD: 73 yrs; Stroke: 74 yrs; Diabetes: 68 yrs). County-specific ages at death are not available from CDC WONDER for Isabella County because cause-specific death counts fall below the suppression threshold (<10) for several conditions.

3.3 YLD calculation

YLD was estimated using the prevalence-based approach:

YLD = P × DW (3)

where P = prevalent cases and DW = internationally validated disability weight (IHME/WHO 2023). This approach does not account for severity distributions, comorbidity, or uncertainty in the disability weight. An incidence-based YLD (YLD = incidence × DW × duration) would be preferable for conditions with well-characterized natural histories but requires incidence data not readily available at the county level.

3.4 Economic burden estimation

We present two economic burden estimates to reflect their different conceptual frameworks. The human capital estimate captures only indirect productivity losses. The VSL estimate reflects societal willingness to pay to prevent premature deaths and is methodologically aligned with HHS regulatory guidelines. The 5.8× difference between central estimates is expected and does not indicate an error; it reflects the different questions each method answers.

4. Analytic Considerations and Potential Biases

4.1 Mortality rate type (age-adjusted vs crude)

County health profiles commonly display age-adjusted mortality rates, which are standardized to a reference population age structure to enable valid geographic comparisons. However, if the MDHHS 2020 PCNA rates are age-adjusted, then converting them to estimated death counts by multiplying by county population is not mathematically valid — age-adjusted rates are not equivalent to crude rates and cannot be directly translated to counts without age-specific denominators. We present the resulting death count estimates but flag this ambiguity. The MDHHS PCNA documentation does not specify rate type for all displayed indicators. This should be resolved before any publication-grade use of these figures.

4.2 Mortality comparison arithmetic

The relative gap between Isabella County total mortality and comparison geographies is: vs. Michigan: (819.9 − 783.1) / 783.1 = 4.7%; vs. United States: (819.9 − 723.6) / 723.6 = 13.3%. Earlier versions of this analysis used incorrect percentages (5% and 87% respectively) that overstated the comparison. The corrected values are used throughout this version.

4.3 Poverty rate discrepancy across sources

Three different poverty estimates appear for Isabella County depending on the source: MDHHS 2020 PCNA reports 26.5% below 100% FPL; Census ACS 5-year estimate (2024, DataUSA): 21.9%; Census QuickFacts (2020–2024 pooled ACS): 19.0%. These differences are attributable to: (a) different ACS reference years and vintages; (b) different treatment of college students — CMU enrollment (~14,000+) can substantially inflate poverty counts under standard ACS methodology, which does not exclude students living away from their parental household; (c) possible differences in poverty universe definitions across ACS products. These figures are not directly comparable. The most recent and broadly accessed Census benchmark is 19.0–21.9%, which is still materially above the state average.

4.4 Psychiatrist shortage ratio interpretation

The HRSA shortage ratio of 176,938:1 for mental health professionals is a designation metric, not a simple county headcount. It is based on full-time-equivalent (FTE) provider counts weighted by service-area population, part-time adjustments, and potentially a multi-county catchment area (Central Michigan Service Area). This ratio exceeds the county's total population of 64,565 because the denominator reflects HRSA's administrative service-area construction, not the county boundary. The metric should be interpreted as an indicator of severe shortage designation status, not as a literal one-provider-per-176,938-persons county ratio.

4.5 County rank disambiguation

Two rank metrics from MDHHS 2020 appear in this analysis: Health Status Rank (50 of 83) and Overall Rank (48 of 83). These are distinct composite indices. The Health Status Rank combines health outcome indicators (mortality rates, disease prevalence, birth outcomes, etc.). The Overall Rank combines health status with social determinants of health (poverty, food insecurity, housing, etc.). Isabella County ranks higher (closer to 1 = best) on the Overall Rank than on Health Status alone, indicating that its social determinant scores partially offset its health outcome deficits.

6. Discussion

The most prominent findings from this analysis are the severe mental health access deficit, the markedly elevated stroke mortality, and the burden of maternal tobacco use and neonatal abstinence syndrome — all of which are substantially worse than Michigan and national comparators. Each of these represents a potentially tractable target for intervention, as evidence-based programs exist for all three.

The mental health access gap is particularly striking under any definition. Whether one uses the HRSA HPSA designation, the hospitalization rate, or simple provider ratios, Isabella County faces acute shortages of psychiatric care. Telepsychiatry integration within primary care (the collaborative care model) has the strongest evidence base for addressing this gap in rural settings and should be considered a priority for any county health planning initiative.

The stroke mortality gap (49% above Michigan average) is the largest relative excess among all tracked mortality causes. Hypertension is the dominant modifiable risk factor for stroke and is highly amenable to community health worker–led management programs. The combination of elevated poverty, uninsured rates, and limited specialty access creates a compounding access barrier that likely explains a substantial portion of this excess.

The poverty data discrepancy across sources (19.0% to 26.5%) is an important analytic caveat rather than a data error. The university student population creates genuine measurement complexity: students may be counted as residents in poverty under ACS methodology but have parental financial support not captured in ACS income questions. Future analyses should attempt to estimate poverty rates excluding the student-age population (18–24 years) separately to better characterize community structural poverty.

7. Recommendations for Future Analysis

To elevate this analysis from a planning approximation to a publication-grade county burden estimate, the following methodological improvements are recommended:

1. Age-specific YLL from CDC WONDER. Query CDC WONDER for Isabella County deaths by 5-year age group and ICD-10 cause, using a 3–5 year pooled period (e.g., 2019–2023) to achieve stable estimates despite small cell sizes. Apply the WHO DALY methodology reference life table to compute age-specific YLL contributions.

2. Post-COVID mortality data. The MDHHS 2020 PCNA predates both the COVID-19 pandemic and significant shifts in opioid overdose mortality. Michigan MDHHS publishes updated county health profiles; 2023–2024 data should be incorporated when available.

3. Severity-stratified YLD. Rather than applying a single moderate-severity disability weight, split prevalent cases into mild/moderate/severe categories using population-level severity distributions from GBD or published literature. This will increase accuracy for heterogeneous conditions (COPD, cancer, diabetes).

4. Uncertainty quantification. Develop probabilistic DALY estimates by propagating uncertainty from each input (mortality rate, prevalence, disability weight) through Monte Carlo simulation. Report 95% uncertainty intervals at the condition level.

5. Poverty population definition. Conduct a sensitivity analysis comparing poverty estimates with and without the 18–24 CMU student population to characterize community structural poverty independently of student poverty.

6. GBD 2023 alignment. GBD 2023 was published in The Lancet in October 2025. Future analyses should verify whether disability weights or the reference life expectancy changed between WHO DALY methodology and GBD 2023 and update accordingly.
