Health Burden from Non-Communicable Diseases, Substance Use Disorders, and Mental Health Conditions in Isabella County, Michigan: A Mixed-Source Modeled Estimate

Cross-sectional analysis for community health planning, 2026
Sergey Soshnikov, MD, PhD1,2
1 Department of Public Health, Herbert H. and Grace A. Dow College of Health Professions, Central Michigan University, Mount Pleasant, MI 48859
2 Soshnikov, Michigan
Correspondence: ssoshmail@gmail.com
Version 3.0 (Audit-Revised) June 2026 Not peer-reviewed Planning purposes only Michigan LE 78.6 yrs (MDHHS 2024)
Abstract

Background. County-level disease burden estimates are rarely available in the United States outside large academic centers. This analysis applies standard DALY methodology to estimate disease burden of seven major condition groups in Isabella County, Michigan — a rural county of 64,565 residents with a designated Medical Underserved Area status and multiple Health Professional Shortage Area (HPSA) designations.

Methods. We constructed disability-adjusted life year (DALY) estimates from secondary data: Michigan MDHHS 2020 PCNA (mortality rates), CDC PLACES/BRFSS (prevalence), WHO/IHME 2023 (disability weights), and U.S. Census ACS 2024 (population denominators). YLL used Michigan observed life expectancy of 78.6 years (MDHHS 2024, avg Male 76.2 / Female 80.9) — not the WHO/IHME normative standard of 88.9 years — as this is more methodologically appropriate for local planning. Economic burden was quantified by human capital (DALYs × Michigan GDP per capita) and value of statistical life (HHS 2026, income-adjusted).

Results. Total estimated burden: 7,696 DALYs (±20%), comprising 3,286 YLL + 4,410 YLD. Leading conditions: cancer (1,585), substance use disorders (1,491), cardiovascular disease (1,205). Stroke mortality is 49% above Michigan average. Economic burden: ~$477M/year (human capital) to ~$4.1B/year (VSL).

Limitations. Mixed-source modeled planning scenario — not a validated county burden estimate. Several mortality values are state-level proxies (county suppressed in CDC WONDER). Prevalence inputs are modeled. YLL uses simplified mean-age approach. Results are directional planning approximations only.

Conclusion. Isabella County's burden is driven by poverty, mental health access deficits, tobacco, and substance use. Findings support telepsychiatry, hypertension control, cessation programs, and prenatal SUD treatment as priorities. See ROI analysis → for investment return estimates.

Keywords: disability-adjusted life years · disease burden · rural health · Michigan · DALY methodology · public health planning · health professional shortage areas

1. Background and Study Context

Isabella County is a rural county in mid-Michigan (FIPS 26073), home to Central Michigan University (CMU) in Mount Pleasant. Population: 64,565 (Census ACS 2024). The presence of a large university creates an unusual demographic: substantial young adult population and elevated poverty rates under standard definitions that do not distinguish student poverty from structural community poverty.

Federal access designations: Primary Care HPSA (low-income), Dental HPSA (low-income), Mental Health HPSA (Central Michigan Service Area, multi-county), and Medically Underserved Area/Population (MUA/P) for low-income residents.

MDHHS 2020 PCNA ranks: Overall Rank 48 of 83 Michigan counties; Health Status Rank 50 of 83 (rank 1 = best). These are distinct indices — see Section 4.5. All MDHHS PCNA data are pre-pandemic baseline; post-COVID values may differ substantially.

Important framing: This document is a mixed-source modeled planning scenario. It pools data from MDHHS 2020, WHO/IHME 2023, County Health Rankings 2025, and Census ACS 2024–25. It should not be cited as an official county burden estimate. A publication-grade analysis requires age-specific CDC WONDER death distributions, direct county prevalence, and systematic uncertainty quantification (see Section 7).

2. Data Sources

2.1 Mortality data

Cause-specific mortality rates from MDHHS 2020 PCNA (Isabella County Profile, Rank 48). Rates are per 100,000 population, pre-pandemic. The PCNA does not specify age-adjusted vs. crude for all indicators — see Section 4.1 for the implications.

County mortality data for SUD, suicide, and several other causes are suppressed in CDC WONDER (cell count <10). Michigan state rate (29.3/100k) was substituted for SUD. Suicide YLL = 0 in this analysis. All substitutions are explicitly flagged in results.

2.2 Prevalence data

In order of priority: CDC PLACES (county model-based estimates for NCD indicators); BRFSS state-level (SUD); SAMHSA national estimates (MH, SUD); MDHHS PCNA figures. CDC PLACES uses multilevel regression and poststratification — all prevalence inputs are classified as modeled, not directly observed.

2.3 Disability weights and life expectancy

Disability weights: WHO/IHME 2023 (GBD 2021 cycle, published Oct 2025 in The Lancet). Moderate-severity weights applied as primary estimates.

Reference life expectancy used for YLL: 78.6 years (Michigan 2024: Male 76.2, Female 80.9, average). Source: MDHHS Vital Statistics. lifeUSMI.asp →

Why Michigan LE, not WHO/IHME 88.9 yrs? The WHO/IHME standard (88.9 yrs, derived from Japanese life tables) is a normative maximum designed for global comparability. Using it for a local Michigan analysis inflates YLL by counting years Michigan residents never realistically live to. Michigan's observed LE (78.6 yrs) gives a more honest estimate of years actually lost — appropriate for local planning and policy. Using 88.9 would yield 11,373 total DALYs vs. 7,696 with 78.6 — a 48% difference that changes condition rankings.

Cancer exception: No single aggregate DW exists for all cancers. Weighted composite DW ≈ 0.30 constructed from MDHHS 2020 site distribution: lung (0.279), colorectal (0.167), prostate (0.126), breast (0.049).

2.4 Population denominators

Total population 64,565 (Census ACS 5-yr 2024). For conditions where CDC PLACES reports adult-only prevalence (COPD, stroke, mental health), adult population ~52,000 (≥18 yrs, Census ACS 2024) was used as the base. For population-wide conditions (diabetes, cancer, SUD), full 64,565 was used.

2.5 Economic parameters

Human capital: DALYs × $62,000 (Michigan GDP per capita 2024). VSL: HHS ASPE 2026 (central $13.4M), income-adjusted for Isabella County using elasticity 0.4 (Viscusi & Aldy 2003): $13.4M × (55,237/80,000)^0.4 = $11.6M per statistical life. See ROI analysis for intervention-level return estimates.

3. Analytic Methods

3.1 DALY framework

Standard WHO DALY formula, no age-weighting, no discounting:

DALY = YLL + YLD (1)

3.2 YLL calculation

YLL = Deaths × (L − Ā) (2)

L = 78.6 years (Michigan LE 2024, MDHHS); Ā = mean age at death (condition-specific national proxy). Deaths estimated as rate/100,000 × population.

YLL simplification. Standard GBD YLL = Σa d(a) × ℓ(a) (age-specific deaths × remaining LE from reference table). The formula above assumes all deaths in a cause group occur at a single mean age — a planning approximation. CDC WONDER can provide age-specific county deaths for a proper computation. Future analyses should use that approach. Mean ages used: CVD 72 yrs; Cancer 68 yrs; SUD 45 yrs; COPD 73 yrs; Stroke 74 yrs; Diabetes 68 yrs (national CDC WONDER 2019–21 aggregates; county-specific values suppressed).

3.3 YLD calculation

YLD = P × DW (3)

P = prevalent cases; DW = WHO/IHME disability weight (moderate severity). Does not account for severity distributions, comorbidity adjustment, or uncertainty intervals. Incidence-based YLD would be preferable for conditions with well-characterized duration data but requires county-level incidence unavailable here.

3.4 Economic burden

Two methods with different conceptual frameworks: Human Capital captures productivity losses only; VSL reflects societal willingness to pay to prevent deaths. The ~6× difference between central estimates is methodologically expected, not an error. For grant applications use VSL (aligned with HHS/OMB); for academic GBD comparison use Human Capital.

4. Analytic Considerations

4.1 Mortality rate type — age-adjusted vs. crude

If MDHHS PCNA mortality rates are age-adjusted (common for geographic comparison dashboards), converting them to death counts by multiplying by county population is not mathematically valid — age-adjusted rates use a reference population structure, not the actual county structure. Death count estimates should be treated as indicative only. The PCNA documentation does not specify rate type for all indicators; this should be resolved before publication-grade use.

4.2 Mortality comparison arithmetic

Isabella vs. Michigan: (819.9 − 783.1) / 783.1 = +4.7%. Isabella vs. US: (819.9 − 723.6) / 723.6 = +13.3%. Earlier versions of this analysis used incorrect values (5% and 87% respectively), now corrected.

4.3 Poverty rate discrepancy

Three estimates exist for Isabella County: MDHHS 2020 PCNA 26.5% below 100% FPL; Census ACS 5-yr 2024: 21.9%; Census QuickFacts 2020–24: 19.0%. Sources differ in ACS vintage, reference year, and student-population treatment. CMU's ~14,000+ enrollment substantially inflates standard ACS poverty counts (students counted as residents in poverty without capturing parental support). All three estimates remain well above Michigan average; the range reflects measurement differences, not contradictions.

4.4 Psychiatrist shortage ratio

The 176,938:1 ratio is a HRSA HPSA designation metric — FTE-weighted for the Central Michigan Service Area (multi-county). It exceeds Isabella County's total population (64,565) because the denominator is an administrative service-area construction, not a county headcount. Interpret as a severe shortage designation indicator, not a literal one-psychiatrist-per-176,938 county ratio.

4.5 County rank disambiguation

Health Status Rank 50/83: composite of health outcome indicators (mortality rates, disease prevalence, birth outcomes). Overall Rank 48/83: combines health status with social determinants (poverty, food insecurity, housing). These are distinct metrics from the same MDHHS 2020 PCNA — do not average or conflate them.

6. Discussion

The most prominent findings are: (1) severe mental health access deficit under any measurement approach; (2) stroke mortality 49% above Michigan average — the largest relative gap among tracked conditions; (3) substantial maternal tobacco and prenatal opioid burden predicting intergenerational health effects. All are tractable with evidence-based interventions.

Using Michigan's observed life expectancy (78.6 yrs) rather than the WHO/IHME normative standard (88.9 yrs) substantially changes rankings. Under local LE, substance use disorders rise to #2 because SUD kills at ~45 years (33.6 years remaining) while cardiovascular disease kills at ~72 (only 6.6 years remaining). This ranking is more policy-relevant for local planning because it prioritizes conditions causing the most years lost relative to what residents actually live.

The poverty data discrepancy (19–26.5%) reflects real measurement complexity from CMU's student population, not data error. Analysts should consider a sensitivity analysis excluding the 18–24 age group to better characterize structural community poverty.

7. Recommendations for Future Analysis

1. Age-specific YLL. Query CDC WONDER for Isabella County deaths by 5-year age group and ICD-10 cause (3–5 year pool, e.g., 2020–2024). Apply WHO reference life table for proper age-specific YLL.

2. Post-COVID mortality update. MDHHS 2020 predates pandemic and the recent opioid mortality surge. Incorporate 2023–2024 county data when available.

3. Severity-stratified YLD. Split prevalence into mild/moderate/severe using GBD severity distributions instead of single moderate-severity DW.

4. Uncertainty quantification. Monte Carlo propagation of input uncertainty (mortality rate, prevalence, DW) to produce 95% uncertainty intervals at condition level.

5. Student population sensitivity. Poverty analysis with and without 18–24 CMU population to separate student vs. structural community poverty.

6. GBD 2023 alignment. Published Oct 2025 in The Lancet. Verify if disability weights or reference LE changed vs. GBD 2021 and update accordingly.
